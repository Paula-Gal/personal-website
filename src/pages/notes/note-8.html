On being curious across domains

People keep asking me how I went from tech to blood donation. As if curiosity needs a logical career path.

The honest answer is I didn't plan it. I read something about plasma supply chains, found it bizarre that the richest countries in the world can't reliably keep blood on shelves, and couldn't stop pulling the thread. That's it. No master plan. Just a question I couldn't put down.

I think this is how the best work starts: not from expertise, but from noticing. You see something that doesn't make sense, and you care enough to ask why. The domain is almost irrelevant. The muscle is the same: look closely, ask basic questions, resist accepting "that's just how it works" as an answer.

The founders I admire most are not the deepest specialists. They're the ones who carried an idea from one field into another. Airbnb applied design thinking to hospitality. Stripe brought developer experience to payments. The insight was never purely technical. It was a borrowed lens.

Being a generalist gets a bad reputation. People assume it means you're shallow. But there's a difference between knowing a little about everything and being able to connect things that others don't. The second one requires going deep enough in multiple areas to see the patterns between them.

Right now I'm reading about behavioral psychology, supply chain logistics, and community design. None of those are "my field." All of them are shaping how I think about donor retention. I wouldn't trade that cross-pollination for any amount of specialization.

Stay curious about things that aren't your job. Eventually, they become your edge.
